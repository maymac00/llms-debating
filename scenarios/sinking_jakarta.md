# Policy Brief: Allocating Jakarta's Contested Adaptation Increment

Jakarta, 2026. The city is sinking faster than the sea is rising. Absolute
sea-level rise off Jakarta runs at about **4.2 mm per year**, but land
subsidence in North Jakarta runs at **7.5 to 10 cm per year**, with pockets up
to **28 cm**. Subsidence is projected to account for up to **85% of relative
sea-level rise by 2050** along more than 75% of Java's northern coastline. Over
**40% of North Jakarta already lies below sea level**, and as the land sinks the
city's 13 rivers lose the gradient to drain into the bay, creating a "bowl" that
traps monsoon runoff inland.

The driver is documented: decades of unregulated groundwater extraction
collapsing the aquifer system, sustained because the public utility **PAM Jaya**
covered only a fraction of the metropolis (**69.53% in 2024**) and could not
penalise pumping while offering no piped alternative. **Governor's Regulation No.
5 of February 2026** now imposes a total ban on commercial and industrial
extraction — enforceable only to the extent the pipe network reaches users.

The provincial government's **base adaptation programme for the coming year is
fixed and is not under discussion.** What is under discussion: the province's
**discretionary adaptation increment** must be allocated across the candidate
lines below. **The absolute size of the increment is fixed and not the object of
deliberation. Argue in percentage shares, and in terms of who is protected and
who is left exposed.**

## Candidate lines (documented facts from the city dossier)

1. **Outer Sea Wall (NCICD Stages B/C).** A 32-km offshore dike enclosing
   Jakarta Bay, **$8–10 billion** for the initial segment, groundbreaking set
   for September 2026; framed as the only structure that can defend the
   below-sea-level bowl against a storm surge. Dossier caveats: it does not
   address subsidence — **if the land keeps sinking behind it, the wall settles
   unevenly and risks structural failure** — and enclosing the bay without first
   cleaning the 13 rivers risks a toxic, low-oxygen retention lagoon.

2. **PAM Jaya piped-water acceleration.** Expanding municipal coverage from
   **90% (end-2026 target) toward 100% (2029–30)** — the precondition for
   enforcing the groundwater ban that drives subsidence, i.e. the only line that
   attacks the root cause. Dossier caveats: **46.2% of treated water is lost to
   leakage, theft, or metering before reaching consumers (non-revenue water)**;
   replacing pipes means excavating thousands of kilometres of road across a
   traffic-choked city; and it delivers **no protection against a surge in the
   interim**.

3. **Coastal mangrove restoration and nature-based recharge.** Decentralised:
   mangrove belts to absorb wave energy, urban green space for natural aquifer
   recharge, household rainwater harvesting to cut reliance on centralised
   utilities. Lower capital outlay, distributed benefit. Dossier caveats: **no
   documented protection figures**; slow to mature; **cannot defend the 40%+ of
   North Jakarta already below sea level against high tides now**.

4. **Household elevation grants for informal settlements.** Extending the
   floor- and roof-lifting grants (**~$1,418 per household**) to the most
   exposed communities — Muara Angke, Muara Baru — who lack grid access and sit
   directly in the tidal zone. Reaches the worst-off directly. Dossier caveats:
   the **co-financing burden is prohibitive** for families already spending
   **~20,000 rupiah per day on jerrycan water against a ~3 million rupiah
   monthly income**; it protects dwellings, not livelihoods, and adds no
   drainage.

5. **Stage A dike reinforcement.** Raising and reinforcing **30–45 km** of
   existing shoreline dikes — the near-term defence (target already slipped from
   2028 to 2030). Fast and visible. Dossier caveat: **localised walls divert
   floodwaters into adjacent low-income neighbourhoods**, transferring the hazard
   rather than removing it.

6. **Managed-retreat pathway toward Nusantara.** Redirect the increment to
   relocating the most exposed residents off the sinking bowl rather than
   defending it, aligned with the capital's move to East Kalimantan. Dossier
   caveats: Nusantara is itself underfunded (allocation slashed to **£300 million
   for 2026**), houses only **~2,000 civil servants against a 1.2 million
   target**, and risks becoming a "ghost city"; its construction has already
   driven **781,000 hectares of deforestation** and the relocation of **35
   ancestral graves**; retreat severs communities from livelihoods and place.

**No figures beyond those above are documented. Do not invent unit costs,
per-line totals, or protection counts.**

## Factors that pull in different directions

- The poorest residents already pay the highest price for water and live in the
  most hazardous, off-grid zones; wealthy enclaves in North Jakarta self-insulate
  with private flood walls and remain dry.
- Governments are systematically drawn to **legible** projects — visible
  seawalls and greenfield capitals — over the structurally invisible work of
  fixing leaks and prosecuting illegal extraction.
- The response will be judged on several measures at once — the number of people
  removed from inundation risk, the cost per person protected, whether the
  hardest-hit and below-sea-level are included, whether harm is redistributed to
  the periphery (sand mining, diverted floodwater, displaced fishers), and
  whether the chosen line is **durable** (addresses subsidence) or merely
  **deliverable** before the next monsoon — and these measures do not point the
  same way.

## Task

Propose an allocation of the discretionary increment in **percentage shares
across at most three** of the six lines (your proposal), and justify the
allocation — and the exclusions — from the standpoint of your conceptual
framework, including, explicitly, **what your framework says to those your
allocation leaves exposed**.
