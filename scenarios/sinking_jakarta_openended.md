# Policy Scenario: Land Subsidence and Coastal Risk in Jakarta

Jakarta, 2026. The city is sinking faster than the sea is rising. Absolute
sea-level rise off the coast runs at about **4.2 mm per year**, but land
subsidence in North Jakarta runs at **7.5 to 10 cm per year**, with pockets up
to **28 cm** — up to an order of magnitude larger than the ocean signal.
Contemporary subsidence is projected to dominate relative sea-level rise along
more than **75% of Java's northern coastline**, accounting for up to **85% of
relative rise by 2050**. Over **40% of North Jakarta already lies below sea
level**. As the land sinks, the city's **13 rivers** lose the hydraulic gradient
to discharge into the bay, creating a "bowl" in which monsoon runoff — **2,000
to 4,000 mm of rain between December and February** — accumulates inland,
compounding riverine and tidal flooding.

The principal driver is documented: decades of largely unregulated groundwater
extraction from deep and shallow aquifers, which irreversibly compacts the
sediment and collapses the ground. This persisted because the public utility
**PAM Jaya** reached only **69.53% of the metropolis in 2024**, so authorities
could not credibly penalise pumping while offering no piped alternative.
**Governor's Regulation No. 5 of February 2026** now imposes a total ban on
commercial and industrial extraction — but it is enforceable only where the pipe
network reaches users. PAM Jaya targets **90% coverage by end-2026** and **100%
by 2029–30**, against a standing constraint that **46.2% of treated water is
lost to leakage, theft, or metering before it reaches consumers**.

The state's headline response is the **National Capital Integrated Coastal
Development (NCICD)** sea wall: a **$8–10 billion** initial offshore segment with
groundbreaking set for September 2026, part of a wider framework costed between
**$40 billion (Jakarta phase)** and **over $80 billion (700-km coastal
extension)**. The documented engineering objection is that a barrier does not
address subsidence: **if the land keeps sinking behind the wall, the structure
settles unevenly and risks failure**, and enclosing the bay without first
cleaning the 13 rivers risks a toxic, low-oxygen retention lagoon. In parallel,
the national capital is being relocated to **Nusantara** in East Kalimantan;
that programme's funding has been cut to **£300 million for 2026**, it houses
only **~2,000 civil servants against a 1.2 million target**, and its
construction has driven **781,000 hectares of deforestation** and the
relocation of **35 ancestral graves**.

The burden is not evenly borne. Wealthy enclaves in North Jakarta self-insulate
with private flood walls and raised foundations and remain dry. Informal
settlements such as **Muara Angke and Muara Baru** sit directly in the tidal
zone, lack grid access, and buy water by the jerrycan — roughly **20,000 rupiah
per day against a ~3 million rupiah monthly income**. Governments are
systematically drawn to **legible** projects — visible seawalls, a greenfield
capital — over the structurally invisible work of fixing leaks and prosecuting
illegal extraction.

## The decision

The provincial government holds a **fixed discretionary adaptation budget of Rp
5 trillion (approximately $310 million) for the coming year**. It is a fraction
of any single mega-structure and cannot be topped up; whatever is not addressed
within it is left to the base programme or left undone.

The response will be judged on several measures at once — the number of people
removed from inundation risk, the cost per person protected, whether the
hardest-hit and the below-sea-level communities are included, whether harm is
redistributed to the periphery (sand mining, diverted floodwater, displaced
fishers), and whether the chosen approach is **durable** (addresses the
subsidence driver) or merely **deliverable** before the next monsoon. These
measures do not point the same way.
