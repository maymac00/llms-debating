# Policy Scenario: The Johannesburg Jobs Crisis

Johannesburg, 2026. The city is the financial heart of South Africa —
contributing roughly **15% of national GDP**, home to **5.9 million people** in
2.29 million households, and holding the country's largest working-age
population at **4.3 million**. It cannot put more than half of that workforce to
work. Official metropolitan unemployment rose from **33.8% in the fourth
quarter of 2025 to 34.7% in the first quarter of 2026**; the expanded measure,
which counts discouraged work-seekers who have given up looking, stands at
**38.4%**.

The arithmetic is structural and adverse. In the first quarter of 2026 the city
created a net **23,000 jobs** while the active labour force expanded by
**76,000** — a shortfall of more than fifty thousand in a single quarter — so
the unemployment rate climbed *even as jobs were added*. Economic growth has
languished below **2% a year**, too slow to absorb a working-age population
swollen by continuous in-migration to the commercial capital of Southern
Africa. This is, first and foremost, a crisis of insufficient labour demand, not
merely of an under-prepared workforce.

The economy that does exist has polarised. Gauteng deindustrialised across the
last decade, shedding traditional manufacturing while high-skill financial and
community services expanded. The first quarter of 2026 alone saw construction
lose **110,000 jobs** and community and social services lose **206,000** — the
latter driven by fiscal consolidation and the termination of short-term
contracts — partly offset by counter-cyclical gains in mining and manufacturing.
The fastest-growing employment, in the logistics corridors linking Johannesburg
to Ekurhuleni and Tshwane, runs heavily on precarious, low-wage, algorithmically
managed shift work that concentrates marginalised Black African youth and women
in the most unstable roles.

Because the formal sector cannot generate jobs at the required scale, the
informal economy has become the fallback for millions: formal contracted
employment grew just **1.1% over fifteen years** while informal, self-employed
and subsistence work grew **108%**, and about **41% of the national workforce**
now operates outside formal contractual arrangements. That sector is itself
constrained — by permit delays, bylaw harassment, eviction, competition from
formal chains expanding into townships, and the absence of basic electricity,
water, sanitation and secure storage in most informal markets.

The crisis falls unequally along three intersecting lines. **Race:** Black
Africans constitute 81% of the population and their youth aged 15–24 face
**66% unemployment**, against **19%** for white youth of the same age — a gap
that has widened since 2008. **Gender:** women's expanded unemployment in
Gauteng is **45%** to men's **33%**; they absorbed **77% of net layoffs** in
early 2025 and earn a median **82%** of the male wage. **Space:** the apartheid
settlement pattern stranded the low-skilled on the urban periphery, far from
where work is generated. The northern suburbs (Sandton, Midrand) hold expanded
unemployment below **20%**; the southern hotspots — Orange Farm, Sebokeng,
Katlehong, Tembisa, Mabopane — exceed **80%**. Peripheral residents spend up to
**50% of per-capita household income** on transport to reach or search for work,
against a 10% affordability benchmark, and fewer than **40% of township
households** have a home internet connection through which to find or apply for
the jobs that do appear. Education does not reliably break the trap: graduate
unemployment has doubled in sixteen years and exceeds **30%** in several fields,
while probit analyses find soft skills — leadership, networking, problem-solving
— stronger predictors of stable employment than narrow technical training.

The response will be mounted from within a fiscal and governance crisis, not
from above it. The mayoralty changed hands more than ten times between 2016 and
2024; in 2024/25 the city wrote off **R24.5 billion** in irregular, fruitless
and unauthorised expenditure. Capital expenditure collapsed from **17% of
spending in 2010 to 7% in 2025**, leaving a **R220 billion** infrastructure
backlog and **R5.2 billion** owed to Eskom; frequent water outages and blackouts
are driving down investor confidence and accelerating business closures. The
**R97.1 billion** 2026/27 budget was declared "unfunded" and "unlawful" by the
Finance Minister, weighed down by a **R10.3 billion** wage agreement the city
cannot meet.

## Factors that pull in different directions

- No realistic budget can close a gap of tens of thousands of net new
  job-seekers per quarter; a response can shape *who* is reached and *how
  durably*, not eliminate the deficit.
- An allocation that maximises headline job numbers and one that reaches the
  worst-off — the southern hotspots, Black African youth, women — need not be the
  same allocation.
- Visible, ribbon-cutting interventions and the slow, invisible work of fixing
  the operating environment for existing employers rarely coincide; governments
  are systematically drawn to the former.
- The money will be spent by an administration with a documented record of
  waste and instability. The response is constrained *within* that reality, not
  outside it.
- Comparable interventions have ranged from the order of **tens of thousands of
  rand per placement** for short-term public-employment stipends to the order of
  **a million rand per permanent job** for industrial development — so the same
  budget buys radically different reach depending on the instrument chosen.

## Budget

You have a fixed discretionary employment budget of **R2 billion** for the
coming year, additional to the city's existing committed programmes. It cannot
be exceeded. Set against **1,030,000 unemployed persons** in the city — under
two thousand rand each — it cannot fund a comprehensive response, and any
serious proposal must therefore concentrate rather than spread.
