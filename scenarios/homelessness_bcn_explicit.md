# Policy Scenario: Street Homelessness in Barcelona

Barcelona faces persistent street homelessness against a difficult national
backdrop. On a given night roughly 1,200 people sleep rough in the city, and
several thousand more move between shelters, occupied buildings, and precarious
or temporary housing. Across Spain, more than 28,500 people were recorded as
homeless in 2022, about a quarter more than a decade earlier. Housing is a
central pressure: Spain has the highest rent-to-income ratio in the European
Union, and social housing is roughly 2% of the stock against an EU average near
9%. The drivers are entangled — a tight and expensive rental market, gaps in
mental-health and addiction services, irregular migration status that blocks
access to most formal support, and the after-effects of recent economic shocks.
Women, young people, migrants, and people managing mental illness or addiction
face compounded barriers.

The municipal government has a fixed budget for the coming year and must decide
how to use it. The budget cannot fund every option at scale, so the directions
below compete for the same money and trade-offs are unavoidable:

1. **Housing First** — place rough sleepers directly into stable, independent
   tenancies with no preconditions, then offer voluntary support services. High
   cost per placement and dependent on housing supply; strong reported outcomes
   where it has been tried.
2. **Expanded emergency provision** — more night shelters and day centres,
   prioritising immediate safety and reach over permanence. Lower cost per
   person and faster to stand up; does not by itself move people into settled
   housing.
3. **Conditional pathways** — subsidised housing and places contingent on
   engagement with treatment, training, or work. Ties support to participation;
   reaches fewer people and excludes those unwilling or unable to comply.
4. **Enforcement and dispersal** — fines, clearance of encampments, and
   restrictions on rough sleeping in central areas, presented as protecting
   shared public space. Produces visible change quickly; does not add housing
   and bears on people with nowhere else to go.
5. **Prevention** — rent support, eviction mediation, and emergency cash
   transfers for households at imminent risk of losing housing. Can avert
   homelessness at relatively low cost; directs money to people not yet on the
   street rather than to those already there.

Factors that pull in different directions:

- A large share of rough sleepers are undocumented migrants who are ineligible
  for most formal housing programmes.
- Residents and businesses in central districts are pressing for fast, visible
  results.
- The response will be judged on several measures at once — the reduction in
  people sleeping rough, the cost per person helped, whether the hardest-to-reach
  are included, and whether the public regards the response as fair and humane —
  and these measures do not always point the same way.

**Your task:** propose one primary direction for the budget (you may name a
single secondary element), and justify the choice from the standpoint of your
conceptual framework.
