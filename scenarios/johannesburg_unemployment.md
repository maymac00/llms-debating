# Policy Brief: Allocating Johannesburg's Discretionary Employment Increment

Johannesburg, 2026. The city is the financial heart of South Africa — roughly
**15% of national GDP**, a population of **5.9 million**, and the country's
largest working-age population at **4.3 million** — and it cannot put more than
half of that workforce to work. Official metropolitan unemployment rose from
**33.8% in Q4 2025 to 34.7% in Q1 2026**; the expanded (LU3) rate sits at
**38.4%**. The crisis is one of demand, not only supply: in Q1 2026 the city
created a net **23,000 jobs** while the active labour force expanded by
**76,000** — a shortfall of more than fifty thousand in a single quarter — so
the rate rose *even as jobs were added*.

The burden is documented and uneven. Black African youth aged 15–24 face
**66% unemployment** against **19%** for white youth of the same age; women's
expanded rate in Gauteng is **45%** to men's **33%**, and women absorbed
**77% of net layoffs** in early 2025. The "unemployment hotspots" — Orange Farm,
Sebokeng, Katlehong, Tembisa, Mabopane — record more than **80%** of the active
labour force jobless, while the northern suburbs (Sandton, Midrand) stay below
**20%**. In many of those townships fewer than **40% of households** have a home
internet connection, and peripheral residents spend up to **50% of per-capita
household income** on transport against a **10%** affordability target.

The structural backdrop is fiscal and political. Between 2016 and 2024 the
mayoralty changed hands more than ten times; in 2024/25 the city wrote off
**R24.5 billion** in irregular, fruitless and unauthorised expenditure. Capital
expenditure has collapsed from **17% of spending in 2010 to 7% in 2025**, leaving
a **R220 billion infrastructure backlog** and **R5.2 billion** owed to Eskom. The
**R97.1 billion** 2026/27 budget was flagged by the Finance Minister as
"unfunded" and "unlawful", driven largely by a **R10.3 billion** politically
facilitated wage agreement the city cannot afford.

The city's existing employment and economic-development programmes for the coming
year are **fixed and not under discussion.** What is under discussion: a
**discretionary employment increment** must be allocated across the candidate
lines below. **The absolute size of the increment is fixed and not the object of
deliberation. Argue in percentage shares, and in terms of who is reached and who
is left excluded.**

## Candidate lines (documented facts from the city dossier)

1. **Special Economic Zone and industrial-corridor expansion (TASEZ / Tambo
   Springs / Vaal / Gauteng Dry Port).** Industrial platforms to attract foreign
   direct investment and localise component manufacturing. TASEZ Phase 2 is on
   track for **4,000 construction jobs with 70% reserved for local township
   residents**; the planned Gauteng Dry Port projects **up to 50,000 permanent
   jobs at R50 billion** (~R1 million per job). Dossier caveats: capital-intensive
   with multi-year lead times — most jobs land years out, not this year;
   construction jobs are temporary; benefits concentrate in the
   Joburg–Ekurhuleni–Tshwane logistics corridor, **not** the southern hotspots;
   it depends on the electricity and water reliability the city has failed to
   maintain; and the logistics boom it feeds runs on precarious, algorithmically
   managed, low-wage shift labour that reinforces existing racial and gendered
   divisions.

2. **Public employment activation (Nasi iSpani model).** A **R1.5 billion**
   Labour Activation Programme transitioning **over 30,000 unemployed youth**
   (~R50,000 per placement) into public-service training and work — infrastructure
   repair, maintenance, community development. The fastest direct route to a
   stipend in hand. Dossier caveats: these are **short-term contracts** — the same
   category whose termination cut **206,000 community-and-social-services jobs in
   Q1 2026**; it creates no durable private-sector demand; it risks churning the
   same youth through successive temporary placements; and it absorbs the active
   searcher, not the discouraged worker who has already stopped looking.

3. **Spatial connection and the distance tax (transport subsidy +
   transit-oriented development).** Direct public-transport subsidies and
   transit-oriented siting to lift the regressive transport burden — up to **50%
   of income** in peripheral wards — and physically connect job-seekers in Orange
   Farm, Sebokeng and Tembisa to the northern employment core. Dossier caveats:
   **it creates no jobs** — it only lowers the cost of reaching a labour market
   already absorbing far fewer entrants than it sheds; it benefits those still
   actively searching, not the **>40% of township households with no home
   internet** who never see the postings; a subsidy on commuting to jobs that may
   not exist.

4. **Informal-economy support and Opportunity Centres.** Informal,
   self-employed and subsistence work grew **108% over 15 years** while formal
   contracted employment grew **1.1%**; about **41% of the national workforce**
   now operates outside formal contracts. Serviced trading spaces (electricity,
   water, sanitation, storage), permit simplification, protection from eviction
   and supermarket displacement, plus Opportunity-Centre business registration and
   digital-literacy support, would underwrite the sector already absorbing the
   excluded. Dossier caveats: survivalist trading is low-margin and acutely
   exposed to food, fuel and energy inflation; traders compete with formal chains
   expanding into townships; formalisation moves few into contracted employment
   with its protections; it consolidates a low-income fallback rather than building
   a ladder out of it.

5. **Enabling-environment repair (electricity, water, the operating environment
   for employers).** The only line that attacks the documented **upstream** cause
   of business closures and stalled hiring: a **R220 billion** infrastructure
   backlog, **R5.2 billion** Eskom debt, CapEx down to **7%** of spending, and
   the frequent outages and blackouts driving down investor confidence. Dossier
   caveats: it creates **no jobs directly** and yields nothing visible before the
   next electoral cycle; every rand here competes head-on with the **R10.3 billion**
   wage agreement the city already cannot fund; on a budget the minister has
   declared unfunded and unlawful, the durability case means spending the city
   does not have on results no one will see this year.

6. **Demand-aligned skills with a soft-skills core (TVET / NARYSEC reform).**
   Workplace-integrated learning rather than classroom-only training, weighted
   toward the soft skills — leadership, networking, problem-solving — that probit
   analyses identify as **stronger predictors** of stable employment than narrow
   technical training. Targets the educational gradient where incomplete-secondary
   youth face **51.6%** expanded unemployment. Dossier caveats: **graduate
   unemployment has doubled in 16 years and exceeds 30%** in several fields —
   credentials already outrun the jobs, so supply-side training risks certifying
   people for demand that is not there; TVET equipment gaps and stigma persist;
   it is the **slowest to mature** of the six lines.

**No figures beyond those above are documented. Do not invent unit costs,
per-line totals, or job counts where none is given.**

## Factors that pull in different directions

- **It is a demand crisis.** 76,000 new labour-force entrants in one quarter
  against 23,000 net new jobs — no line here closes that gap, and several merely
  redistribute who stands at the front of the queue.
- **The burden is not evenly borne.** An allocation that maximises headline job
  numbers and one that reaches the worst-off — Black African youth at 66%, women
  at 45%, the southern hotspots above 80% — need not coincide.
- **Governments are drawn to the legible.** Ribbon-cutting SEZs and dry ports
  photograph well; keeping the lights on for existing employers does not. The
  visible option and the durable option are rarely the same.
- **The allocator is the problem.** Every rand is spent by an administration that
  wrote off R24.5 billion in a single year and changed mayors more than ten times
  in eight. The deliberation allocates *within*, not outside, that governance
  reality.
- **The criteria conflict.** Total jobs created, cost per job, whether the
  periphery and the discouraged are reached, whether women and Black African youth
  gain proportionally, and whether the result is **durable** or merely
  **deliverable** before the next budget cycle — these do not point the same way.

## Task

Propose an allocation of the discretionary employment increment in **percentage
shares across at most three** of the six lines (your proposal), and justify the
allocation — and the exclusions — from the standpoint of your conceptual
framework, including, explicitly, **what your framework says to those your
allocation leaves exposed**.
