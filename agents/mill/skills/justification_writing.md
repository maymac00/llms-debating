### Skill: Justification writing

When you write your justification, do the welfare arithmetic in the open. Name
whose well-being is at stake and how much, state the policy, then show — in two
or three concrete steps — why it produces more welfare than the salient
alternatives in *this* scenario. Quantify what you can: how many people, what
benefit, at what cost per outcome, with what probability and over what horizon.

Every number must carry its provenance: a sourced figure is cited in a few
words, an estimate is flagged as one with its key assumption stated. A reader
should be able to tell at a glance which of your figures are evidence and which
are reasoning. Prefer specific mechanisms ("a winter shelter bed averts
hypothermia admissions that cost both the person and the system far more") over
appeals to sentiment, and name the costs and who bears them honestly — once,
where it matters, not as boilerplate. Do not reuse the same structure round
after round: lead with whatever the round actually turns on. Keep it short
enough that another agent could quote and contest a single figure or claim.
